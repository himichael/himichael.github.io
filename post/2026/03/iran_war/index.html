The 6-Dimension Framework

1. WHAT – Conflict Overview

This dimension focuses on what is happening on the ground. It includes:

  • Real-time military developments
  • Airstrikes, missile exchanges, and battlefield dynamics
  • Key events such as leadership strikes or infrastructure attacks
  • 👉 In essence, it describes the current state and evolution of the conflict.

2. WHY – Causes and Motivations

This dimension explains why the conflict is occurring. It covers:

  • Strategic objectives (e.g., nuclear concerns, deterrence)
  • Security threats and threat perceptions
  • Long-term geopolitical tensions and failed diplomacy
  • 👉 It answers the question: What are the underlying drivers of escalation?

3. HOW – Military Strategy and Tactics

This dimension analyzes how the war is being fought. It includes:

  • Types of warfare (air campaigns, drone warfare, cyber operations)
  • Operational strategies (e.g., decapitation strikes, asymmetric warfare)
  • Target selection (military vs energy infrastructure)
  • 👉 It reveals the methods and logic of military engagement.

4. WHO ELSE – Regional and Global Involvement

This dimension examines who else is involved beyond the main actors. It includes:

  • Proxy groups and allied forces
  • Regional spillover into neighboring countries
  • The role of major powers and international actors
  • 👉 It highlights the expansion of the conflict across actors and regions.

5. SO WHAT – Implications and Impact

This dimension evaluates the consequences of the conflict. It covers:

  • Short-term effects (oil prices, market volatility, security risks)
  • Long-term implications (regional balance of power, global order)
  • Humanitarian and economic consequences
  • 👉 It answers: Why does this conflict matter globally?

6. WHAT NEXT – Future Trajectory

This dimension explores what is likely to happen next. It includes:

  • Possible escalation or de-escalation scenarios
  • Risk of regional war or prolonged stalemate
  • Prospects for negotiation or continued conflict
  • 👉 It provides a forward-looking assessment of the war’s trajectory.

Iran War 13 key points

1. Current Situation of the Iran War

The conflict between Iran and the coalition led by Israel and the United States has entered its third week, with hostilities intensifying across multiple fronts in the Middle East. Military strikes, economic disruption, and mounting geopolitical tensions suggest that the conflict may evolve into a prolonged regional confrontation.

Escalating military operations

Coalition airstrikes have continued to target Iranian military infrastructure, including missile bases and strategic logistics facilities. Recent attacks focused on Kharg Island, a critical hub responsible for a large share of Iran’s oil exports. Washington has indicated that further strikes remain possible if Iranian retaliation persists.

Iran, in response, has launched waves of ballistic missiles and drones against Israeli territory and U.S. military installations throughout the region. These retaliatory attacks have also affected several Gulf states that host American forces, expanding the operational theatre of the war.

Expansion into a regional conflict

The war is increasingly spilling beyond the direct confrontation between the main parties. Iranian-aligned groups and military units have conducted strikes across the Persian Gulf region, including attacks targeting infrastructure and shipping routes in neighboring states.

Several Gulf countries—including Bahrain and Kuwait—have experienced missile interceptions and damage to military facilities connected to U.S. operations. These incidents underscore the growing risk that the conflict could escalate into a wider regional war involving multiple Middle Eastern actors.

Strategic tensions around the Strait of Hormuz

One of the most critical geopolitical flashpoints is the Strait of Hormuz, a narrow maritime corridor through which roughly 20% of global oil supplies normally transit. Since the start of the war, shipping traffic in the strait has been severely disrupted, and several vessels have been damaged or abandoned due to security threats.

Iranian officials have warned that vessels linked to hostile countries could be denied passage, while the United States has urged allied nations to deploy naval forces to secure the route. The uncertainty surrounding this strategic chokepoint has triggered significant volatility in global energy markets.

Economic repercussions

The conflict has already produced substantial economic consequences. Oil prices have surged sharply as traders anticipate potential supply disruptions, particularly if shipping through the Strait of Hormuz remains constrained. Analysts warn that prolonged instability could contribute to global inflationary pressure and financial market volatility.

Diplomatic stalemate

Despite growing international concern, diplomatic efforts to broker a ceasefire remain limited. Political leaders involved in the conflict have adopted increasingly uncompromising positions, and negotiations have yet to yield meaningful progress toward de-escalation.

Short analytical summary

The Iran war has evolved into a complex regional crisis characterized by sustained airstrikes, retaliatory missile attacks, and escalating tensions around vital energy infrastructure.

With the security of the Strait of Hormuz in question and diplomatic channels stalled, the conflict poses significant risks not only to regional stability but also to the global economy.

2.Why the United States and Israel Are Confronting Iran

The current military confrontation between Iran and the U.S.–Israeli coalition is rooted in long-standing geopolitical tensions, security concerns, and strategic competition in the Middle East. Several major factors have contributed to the escalation of hostilities.

Iran’s nuclear program

One of the central issues is Iran’s nuclear program. Both Israel and the United States have long expressed concern that Iran could develop the capability to produce nuclear weapons. Although Iran maintains that its nuclear activities are intended for civilian purposes, Israeli and American officials argue that the program could eventually enable Iran to build a nuclear arsenal.
Israel, in particular, views a potential Iranian nuclear weapon as an existential threat, given the hostile relationship between the two countries and repeated political statements by Iranian leaders criticizing the Israeli state.

Regional power competition

Another major factor is the struggle for influence across the Middle East. Iran has built strong relationships with several regional actors and armed groups that oppose Israel and Western influence. These include organizations such as Hezbollah and Hamas. Israel and the United States argue that Iranian military and financial support for these groups destabilizes the region and threatens Israeli security. As a result, both countries have sought to limit Iran’s regional influence and military capabilities.

Security concerns and military deterrence

Israel has historically adopted a strategy of preventive or preemptive strikes against threats it perceives as potentially catastrophic. From Israel’s perspective, weakening Iran’s missile infrastructure and military facilities may reduce the risk of future large-scale attacks. The United States shares some of these security concerns, particularly regarding the protection of its military bases and allies in the region.

Strategic importance of energy routes

The conflict also intersects with global energy security. Iran sits near the Strait of Hormuz, a critical maritime chokepoint through which a large portion of the world’s oil supply passes. Any escalation involving Iran has the potential to disrupt global energy markets, making the region strategically important not only for Middle Eastern states but also for the broader international community.

Breakdown of diplomacy

For years, international negotiations attempted to limit Iran’s nuclear program through agreements such as the Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action. However, the agreement faced political opposition and eventually collapsed, leading to renewed tensions and sanctions. Without a stable diplomatic framework, military confrontation has increasingly replaced negotiation as the dominant form of pressure between the parties.

Analytical summary:

The conflict between Iran and the U.S.–Israeli coalition is driven by a combination of nuclear proliferation concerns, regional power rivalry, security threats posed by allied militant groups, and the strategic importance of Middle Eastern energy routes. As diplomatic mechanisms have weakened, these tensions have gradually escalated into open military confrontation.

3. Possible Future Scenarios of the Iran War

Scenario One: U.S.–Israel Strategic Victory

A victory for the U.S.–Israeli coalition would likely involve severely weakening Iran’s military capabilities, particularly its missile forces, nuclear infrastructure, and regional proxy networks.

Middle East consequences If this outcome occurred, several major changes could reshape the regional balance of power:

  • Iran’s influence across the Middle East would likely decline significantly.
  • Groups supported by Iran—such as Hezbollah and Hamas—could lose funding, weapons, or strategic support.
  • Israel’s deterrence power would be strengthened, potentially reshaping security arrangements across the region.
  • Some Gulf states might move closer to Israel diplomatically, expanding the normalization trend that began after the Abraham Accords.

Global consequences From a global perspective:

  • The United States would reinforce its reputation as the dominant military power in the international system.
  • Western alliances would appear more cohesive and credible.
  • Oil markets might stabilize if the security of the Strait of Hormuz is restored.

However, such a victory could also produce long-term instability, especially if Iran experiences internal political turmoil or regime crisis.

Scenario Two: Iranian Strategic Success

Iran does not necessarily need to defeat the United States militarily to achieve a strategic victory. Instead, it could succeed by prolonging the conflict, imposing economic costs, and demonstrating that Western military power cannot easily dominate the region.

Middle East consequences If Iran manages to maintain its military capabilities while inflicting substantial costs on its opponents:

  • Iran’s regional prestige could increase among groups that oppose Western influence.
  • Iranian-aligned actors across the region might gain confidence and expand their influence.
  • Israel could face a more persistent and decentralized security threat.

This scenario could also weaken the security architecture built around U.S. partnerships in the Gulf.

Global consequences At the global level:

  • The credibility of U.S. military deterrence could be questioned.
  • Rival powers such as Russia and China might view the outcome as evidence that American influence in the Middle East is declining.
  • Energy markets could remain volatile if regional tensions continue to threaten shipping routes.

Such a shift could accelerate the transition toward a more multipolar international system.

Scenario Three: Prolonged Stalemate

Historically, many Middle Eastern conflicts end not with decisive victory but with prolonged stalemate and uneasy deterrence.

Middle East consequences In this scenario:

  • Neither side achieves decisive strategic objectives.
  • Military clashes continue intermittently through missile strikes, cyber operations, and proxy conflicts.
  • Regional instability becomes a long-term condition rather than a temporary crisis.

Countries across the region would likely invest more heavily in missile defense systems, military modernization, and strategic alliances.

Global consequences Globally, a stalemate could produce several structural effects:

  • Persistent geopolitical risk in energy markets.
  • Continued military presence of major powers in the region.
  • Greater fragmentation of the international order as countries hedge between competing powers.

Strategic Assessment

Most geopolitical analysts consider a clear military victory unlikely because Iran’s strategy relies on asymmetric warfare, regional proxies, and geographic depth. These factors make rapid military defeat difficult even for technologically superior forces.

As a result, the most probable outcome may be a negotiated settlement or an extended stalemate, rather than an outright victory for either side.

Short analytical summary

The future trajectory of the Iran war will significantly influence both the Middle Eastern balance of power and the broader international order. A decisive U.S.–Israeli victory could reinforce Western dominance in the region, while an Iranian strategic success might accelerate the shift toward a more multipolar global system. However, given the complexity of the conflict and the asymmetric capabilities involved, a prolonged stalemate remains one of the most plausible scenarios.

4.Military tactics and war forms

The conflict between Iran and the coalition led by Israel and the United States has evolved into a highly complex, multi-layered military confrontation. The war is no longer defined by conventional battlefield engagements alone, but by a combination of air dominance, asymmetric retaliation, and coordinated multi-front operations.

Air campaign and precision strike strategy

Coalition forces have established a strong advantage in airpower and long-range strike capabilities, conducting sustained operations against Iranian military infrastructure and leadership targets.

Recent strikes have included attacks on critical energy facilities such as the South Pars gas field, as well as targeted killings of senior Iranian officials. These operations demonstrate a decapitation-oriented strategy, aimed at disrupting Iran’s command structure and weakening its ability to coordinate large-scale responses.

In addition, the United States has deployed advanced missile systems and integrated joint operations across air, land, and naval platforms, reflecting a highly coordinated and technology-driven campaign.

Missile and drone warfare

Iran has relied heavily on ballistic missiles and large-scale drone attacks as its primary means of retaliation.

Rather than concentrating forces, Iran has adopted a strategy of dispersed, high-frequency strikes, targeting Israel, U.S. bases, and energy infrastructure across the Gulf. ([Reuters][3])

Recent reports indicate:

  • Widespread missile and drone attacks across multiple countries
  • Damage to oil and gas facilities in the Gulf region
  • Continued ability to sustain attacks despite heavy losses

These operations highlight Iran’s emphasis on volume, persistence, and geographic spread rather than precision superiority.

Asymmetric warfare doctrine

The overall military approach of Iran reflects a classic model of asymmetric warfare, designed to counter a technologically superior opponent.

Key characteristics include:

  • Use of low-cost drones and missiles at scale
  • Targeting of economic and energy infrastructure
  • Avoidance of direct large-scale conventional confrontation

This strategy is rooted in the assumption that a stronger adversary will attempt to dominate through airpower and leadership strikes, and therefore seeks to offset this imbalance through endurance and distributed attacks.

Decapitation strategy and leadership targeting

One of the most defining features of the current phase of the war is the systematic use of decapitation strikes.

Israel has carried out a series of targeted killings against senior Iranian figures, including top political and intelligence officials.

While such operations may temporarily disrupt command structures, analysts warn that they can also:

  • Increase internal radicalization
  • Strengthen hardline factions
  • Prolong the conflict rather than resolve it

Multi-front and multi-theater warfare

The conflict has expanded into a multi-theater war, extending far beyond direct exchanges between Iran and Israel.

Military activity now spans:

  • Iranian territory
  • Israeli territory
  • Gulf states and regional energy hubs
  • Maritime and energy infrastructure zones

Iranian strikes on energy facilities in countries such as Qatar, Saudi Arabia, and the UAE demonstrate a deliberate attempt to expand the operational battlefield and impose regional costs. ([AP News][4])

Short analytical summary

The Iran war has developed into a hybrid military confrontation characterized by sustained air campaigns, large-scale missile and drone warfare, and expanding multi-front operations. While the U.S.–Israeli coalition relies on technological superiority and leadership targeting, Iran has adopted an asymmetric strategy centered on endurance, dispersion, and regional disruption. This combination of competing doctrines is likely to prolong the conflict and increase the risk of broader regional escalation.

5. Cyber Warfare and Information Operations

Cyber warfare operations

Cyber activities have become an integral component of the conflict, operating alongside conventional military strikes.

Coordinated cyber operations—particularly by the United States and Israel—have reportedly disrupted Iranian command, control, and communication networks prior to kinetic attacks, enhancing tactical surprise and operational efficiency.

At the same time, Iran and affiliated groups have launched retaliatory cyberattacks targeting government systems, private companies, and online platforms. These include:

  • Distributed denial-of-service (DDoS) attacks
  • Data-wiping malware targeting critical systems
  • Phishing and infiltration campaigns

Recent incidents demonstrate that cyber warfare is increasingly used not only for espionage, but also for direct operational disruption and strategic signaling.

Information warfare and propaganda

The conflict is also characterized by an intense battle over narratives and public perception.

Iranian state media and affiliated networks have conducted large-scale disinformation campaigns, spreading manipulated images, misleading videos, and exaggerated claims of military success.

Meanwhile, opposing actors have employed their own media strategies, including:

  • Viral propaganda videos and meme-based content
  • Selective framing of battlefield outcomes
  • Amplification of favorable narratives through global media

These efforts aim to shape both domestic morale and international opinion, turning the information space into a parallel battlefield where perception can be as critical as reality.

Infrastructure hacking and system disruption

A key feature of the cyber domain is the targeting of critical infrastructure and civilian systems.

Cyber operations have included:

  • Disruption of financial systems and digital services
  • Intrusions into communication platforms and mobile applications
  • Interference with media broadcasting and public information channels

In some cases, cyberattacks have caused widespread service outages and communication breakdowns, including near-total internet disruptions within Iran during key phases of the الحرب.

These actions demonstrate a shift toward system-level disruption, where digital attacks can produce tangible economic and societal impact comparable to physical strikes.

Psychological operations (PSYOP)

Psychological warfare has emerged as a central objective of cyber and information operations.

Both sides have conducted campaigns designed to influence civilian behavior, undermine trust, and generate fear or confusion. Examples include:

  • Fake messages, calls, and alerts aimed at civilians
  • Hacked platforms used to broadcast political or military messaging
  • Symbolic cyberattacks designed to demonstrate vulnerability

For instance, large-scale phishing and intimidation campaigns targeting civilians have been used to create a sense of insecurity and uncertainty.

More broadly, cyber and information tools are increasingly synchronized with military operations, forming a hybrid warfare model in which psychological impact is as strategically important as physical damage.

Analytical takeaway

Cyber and information warfare have evolved into a decisive dimension of the conflict, enabling both sides to disrupt infrastructure, manipulate narratives, and influence public perception at scale. By integrating digital operations with conventional military strategy, the الحرب demonstrates how modern conflicts are fought simultaneously across physical and cognitive domains.

6. Energy War

Oil and gas infrastructure strikes

Energy infrastructure has emerged as a central target in the conflict, reflecting its strategic importance to both regional economies and global markets.

Military operations have increasingly focused on critical facilities, including oil terminals, gas fields, and export hubs. For example, strikes on major assets such as the South Pars gas field and threats against power plants highlight the vulnerability of energy systems in wartime.

At the same time, Iran has demonstrated its willingness to target energy infrastructure across the region, signaling that escalation could extend beyond national borders. This mutual targeting of energy assets marks a departure from previous norms, where such infrastructure was often avoided due to its global economic significance.

LNG and oil supply disruption

The conflict has caused severe disruptions to global energy supply chains, particularly in oil and liquefied natural gas (LNG).

The closure and instability of key transport routes have significantly reduced exports from the Gulf region. Notably:

  • Around 20% of global oil and LNG supply has been affected by disruptions in the Strait of Hormuz ([Reuters][3])
  • LNG production and export capacity, especially in Qatar, has been partially halted or reduced ([Reuters][4])
  • Shipping delays and insurance risks have further constrained global energy flows

Unlike oil, natural gas markets are less flexible due to limited storage and infrastructure constraints, making LNG particularly vulnerable to prolonged disruption.

Energy weaponization

Energy resources have increasingly been used as a strategic tool to exert pressure on adversaries and the global economy.

Iran has leveraged its position in the global energy system by threatening to:

  • Disrupt regional oil and gas production
  • Attack infrastructure in neighboring countries
  • Restrict access to key export routes

Analysts note that beyond blocking transport routes, the deliberate destruction of energy infrastructure could create long-term supply shortages, amplifying the geopolitical impact of the conflict.

This reflects a broader shift toward “energy weaponization,” where control over production and distribution becomes a means of strategic coercion rather than merely an economic asset.

Strait chokepoint control

Control over maritime chokepoints—particularly the Strait of Hormuz—has become one of the most decisive elements of the energy war.

The strait serves as a critical corridor for global energy trade, and its disruption has had immediate worldwide consequences. Key developments include:

  • A sharp decline in tanker traffic, with shipping activity dropping dramatically
  • Direct attacks on commercial vessels and mining of sea routes
  • Temporary or near-total suspension of transit through the strait

As a result, oil prices have surged and global markets have experienced significant volatility, underscoring the strategic leverage of chokepoint control.

The ability to restrict or reopen such a vital passage effectively grants significant geopolitical influence, making the strait a focal point of both military and economic strategy.

Analytical takeaway

The energy dimension of the conflict illustrates how modern warfare extends beyond the battlefield into global economic systems. By targeting infrastructure, disrupting supply chains, and controlling strategic chokepoints, both sides are leveraging energy as a tool of coercion. This transformation of energy into a strategic weapon underscores the deep interconnection between military conflict and global economic stability.

7. Global Economic Impact

Oil price shock

The conflict has triggered a significant oil price shock, driven primarily by supply disruptions in the Middle East.
With the effective closure of key routes such as the Strait of Hormuz and damage to energy infrastructure, global oil supply has been severely constrained. As a result:

  • Oil prices surged above $100–120 per barrel during peak disruption
  • Up to 15–20% of global oil supply has been affected
  • Energy markets have experienced extreme volatility

This shock is widely regarded as one of the most severe since the 1970s energy crisis, highlighting the continued vulnerability of the global economy to geopolitical disruptions in energy-producing regions.

Inflation transmission

The rise in energy prices has rapidly translated into broader inflationary pressures across global economies.

Higher oil and gas costs increase production and transportation expenses, which are then passed on to consumers. Key transmission effects include:

  • Rising fuel and electricity prices
  • Increased cost of goods and services
  • Pressure on household consumption and real incomes

Economists warn that sustained energy shocks could force central banks to delay interest rate cuts or even tighten monetary policy, complicating economic recovery.

In some scenarios, inflation could rise significantly, with risks of stagflation—a combination of slow growth and persistent inflation.

Supply chain disruption

Global supply chains have been heavily disrupted, particularly due to maritime instability and rising transport costs.

The Strait of Hormuz, a critical shipping route, carries a large share of global trade. Its disruption has led to:

  • Delays in shipping and delivery of goods
  • Sharp increases in freight and insurance costs
  • Shortages of key commodities, including energy and fertilizers

Since fuel accounts for a major portion of shipping costs, rising energy prices have amplified supply chain stress across industries.

More broadly, analysts note that the conflict’s effects are cascading through global trade networks, reshaping supply chains and commodity flows on a potentially long-term basis.

Financial market volatility

Financial markets have reacted sharply to the conflict, reflecting heightened uncertainty and risk aversion.
Key market responses include:

  • Declines in major stock indices (e.g., Dow Jones and S&P 500)
  • Increased volatility in equity and bond markets
  • Capital flows into safe-haven assets such as gold and the U.S. dollar

At the same time, rising energy prices and inflation concerns have complicated monetary policy expectations, adding further instability to global financial systems.

Market volatility reflects not only immediate disruptions but also uncertainty about the duration and escalation of the conflict, making economic forecasting increasingly difficult.

Analytical takeaway

The economic impact of the conflict illustrates how regional wars can generate systemic global consequences. Through oil price shocks, inflation transmission, supply chain disruption, and financial market volatility, the crisis has exposed the deep interdependence of modern economies. As geopolitical instability persists, the risk of prolonged economic turbulence—including stagflation and reduced growth—remains a central concern.

8. Regional Spillover Effects

Proxy warfare

The conflict has significantly intensified through the activation of regional proxy networks, transforming a bilateral confrontation into a broader, multi-actor struggle.

Iran has long relied on a network of allied groups across the Middle East—including militias in Iraq, Lebanon, Syria, and Yemen—to project power indirectly.

Recent developments indicate that:

  • Iran-backed militias in Iraq have escalated attacks on U.S. positions
  • Proxy groups are increasingly targeting regional and international interests
  • The conflict reflects a shift from contained proxy engagement to more direct coordination

This model allows Iran to extend its operational reach while avoiding full-scale direct confrontation, effectively externalizing parts of the conflict across multiple territories.

Hezbollah and militia involvement

One of the most critical dimensions of spillover is the involvement of Hezbollah and other paramilitary groups.

In Lebanon, Hezbollah has engaged in cross-border exchanges with Israel, opening an additional front in the conflict.

At the same time:

  • Iranian-aligned militias across the region have signaled readiness to intervene
  • Some groups have already increased operational activity, while others remain strategically restrained
  • The degree of involvement appears calibrated, balancing escalation with the risk of broader war

Despite signs of caution among certain groups, their presence introduces persistent instability and creates the potential for rapid escalation if coordination intensifies.

Expansion to Gulf states

The conflict has expanded beyond its original actors, directly impacting Gulf states and regional infrastructure.

Iranian retaliatory operations have included missile and drone strikes on countries such as:

  • The United Arab Emirates
  • Qatar
  • Bahrain

These attacks have targeted both military and civilian infrastructure, including airports and energy facilities, demonstrating a deliberate effort to raise the regional cost of the conflict.

As a result, previously peripheral states are increasingly drawn into the conflict, raising the risk of a wider regional war involving multiple sovereign actors.

Cross-border escalation

A defining feature of the current phase is the rapid escalation of cross-border military activity across multiple theaters.

The conflict now spans:

  • Iran and Israel
  • Lebanon (via Hezbollah)
  • Iraq (via militias)
  • Maritime zones such as the Red Sea and Gulf

This multi-directional escalation reflects a breakdown of geographic containment, where actions in one theater trigger responses in others.

For example:

  • Missile exchanges and proxy attacks have occurred simultaneously across several countries
  • Maritime threats in the Red Sea and Gulf regions have expanded the operational scope
  • Regional actors are increasingly involved, either directly or indirectly

Such dynamics illustrate how localized conflicts can evolve into system-wide regional crises, driven by interconnected alliances and strategic dependencies.

Analytical takeaway

The spillover effects of the conflict demonstrate how modern wars rarely remain geographically contained. Through proxy warfare, militia involvement, and cross-border escalation, the confrontation has evolved into a complex regional system of interconnected conflicts. This diffusion of violence not only amplifies instability but also increases the likelihood of unintended escalation, making containment and de-escalation significantly more difficult.

9. Great Power Competition

Role of China

China has adopted a cautious and strategically restrained posture, seeking to balance its economic interests with geopolitical positioning.

While publicly advocating for ceasefire and diplomatic resolution, Beijing has provided limited indirect support to Iran, including technological and logistical assistance that enhances its defensive capabilities.

At the same time:

  • China has avoided direct military involvement to protect its energy imports and global trade interests
  • It has positioned itself as a potential mediator in the conflict
  • It seeks to preserve long-term influence in the Middle East without provoking direct confrontation with the United States

Analysts note that China benefits from a “low-cost engagement strategy”, gaining geopolitical leverage while minimizing exposure to military and economic risks.

Role of Russia

Russia’s involvement has been more operational but still calibrated to avoid escalation.

Moscow has provided intelligence support and limited logistical assistance to Iran, including data on U.S. military movements, enhancing Iran’s targeting capabilities.

Key features of Russia’s role include:

  • Indirect military support rather than direct intervention
  • Diplomatic alignment with China in opposing U.S. actions
  • Strategic use of the conflict to challenge Western influence

However, Russia’s engagement remains constrained by its ongoing commitments elsewhere, particularly in Ukraine, limiting its ability to project full-scale power in the region.

U.S. global leadership credibility

The conflict has become a major test of U.S. global leadership and strategic credibility.

While the United States continues to demonstrate overwhelming military capability, its actions have raised questions among allies and partners regarding:

  • Strategic consistency and long-term objectives
  • The legitimacy of intervention
  • The reliability of U.S. commitments in a rapidly changing global order

Some analysts argue that the war has strained U.S. diplomatic capital and exposed limits to its ability to shape outcomes unilaterally.

At the same time, perceptions in parts of the Global South suggest that U.S. actions may be viewed as destabilizing, potentially weakening its moral authority and soft power.

Multipolar world shift

The conflict reflects and accelerates the broader transition toward a multipolar global system.

Rather than a single dominant power, the international order increasingly consists of multiple competing centers of influence, including the United States, China, and Russia.

Key dynamics include:

  • Growing coordination—though not full alignment—between China and Russia
  • Reduced ability of the U.S. to act as a singular global arbiter
  • Increasing strategic autonomy among regional and middle powers

The war is widely seen as reinforcing global fragmentation, with power distributed across multiple actors pursuing divergent interests.

This shift toward multipolarity does not imply stability; rather, it often leads to greater uncertainty, competition, and the risk of overlapping conflicts.

Analytical takeaway

The Iran conflict illustrates how regional wars are deeply embedded within great power competition. China’s cautious opportunism, Russia’s calibrated support, and the challenges to U.S. leadership collectively reflect an emerging multipolar order. As power becomes more diffuse, global stability increasingly depends on the interaction—and misalignment—of multiple major actors rather than a single dominant force.

10. Diplomacy and International Law

Ceasefire negotiations

Diplomatic efforts to achieve a ceasefire have been ongoing but remain fragile and largely inconclusive.

Recent developments indicate that proposed ceasefire frameworks have faced significant obstacles, as both sides maintain maximalist positions. Iran, for instance, has rejected U.S.-backed proposals and set strict preconditions, including:

  • A complete halt to military strikes
  • Security guarantees against future attacks
  • Compensation for war damages

At the same time, international actors have called for an immediate cessation of hostilities, but negotiations remain stalled due to mutual distrust and incompatible strategic objectives.

Sanctions and economic pressure

Economic sanctions continue to serve as a primary non-military tool of coercion.

Western countries have intensified financial and trade restrictions targeting Iran’s banking system, energy exports, and military supply chains. These measures aim to:

  • Limit Iran’s ability to finance military operations
  • Isolate it from global financial networks
  • Increase domestic economic pressure

Historically, mechanisms such as the “snapback” sanctions framework have enabled rapid reimposition of international sanctions in response to non-compliance, reinforcing their role as a flexible diplomatic instrument.

However, sanctions also carry broader consequences, often affecting civilian populations and complicating humanitarian access, raising ethical and legal concerns.

United Nations involvement

The United Nations has played a central but constrained role in managing the conflict.

The UN Security Council has adopted resolutions condemning specific actions—particularly Iran’s strikes on regional states—while calling for de-escalation and respect for international law.

At the same time:

  • Divisions among major powers (e.g., abstentions by China and Russia) have limited unified action
  • UN agencies have issued warnings about humanitarian risks and regional escalation
  • The organization has struggled to enforce binding resolutions effectively

This reflects a broader structural limitation of the UN system, where geopolitical rivalries often hinder decisive intervention.

Legitimacy of strikes

One of the most contentious issues is the legal legitimacy of military actions under international law.

Legal experts and UN-affiliated bodies have raised serious concerns regarding:

  • The legality of preemptive or preventive strikes
  • The absence of UN Security Council authorization
  • The failure to meet the criteria for self-defense under the UN Charter

Many analyses conclude that certain U.S. and Israeli strikes may constitute violations of international law, potentially qualifying as acts of aggression.

At the same time, retaliatory actions by Iran—particularly those targeting civilian infrastructure or third-party states—have also been widely condemned as unlawful.

This mutual contestation highlights the erosion of clear legal boundaries in modern warfare, where competing interpretations of legality are used to justify military actions.

Analytical takeaway

The diplomatic and legal dimension of the conflict reveals a widening gap between international norms and geopolitical realities. While ceasefire negotiations and sanctions reflect ongoing efforts to manage the crisis, the limited effectiveness of the United Nations and the contested legality of military actions underscore the fragility of the rules-based international order. As legal frameworks are increasingly challenged, diplomacy faces growing difficulty in constraining escalation and restoring stability.

11. Domestic Politics and Regime Stability

Regime stability

Despite intense external pressure and internal strain, the Iranian regime has so far demonstrated a degree of institutional resilience.

Following the assassination of the previous supreme leader, a rapid leadership transition was implemented, with a new supreme leader installed and key political and military elites reaffirming their loyalty.

At the same time:

  • Core state institutions, particularly the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC), remain intact
  • Governance structures continue to function, albeit under strain
  • The regime has prioritized continuity to prevent systemic collapse

However, analysts note that this stability is fragile rather than absolute, heavily dependent on coercive capacity and elite cohesion rather than broad public legitimacy.

Elite fragmentation

The conflict has increased the risk of fragmentation within the ruling elite, particularly between political, clerical, and military factions.

The sudden leadership transition and ongoing military pressure have created uncertainty regarding:

  • Succession legitimacy and internal power distribution
  • The balance of influence between civilian leadership and the IRGC
  • Strategic disagreements over war escalation versus negotiation

While public signs of division remain limited, experts suggest that internal tensions may be growing beneath the surface, especially as the costs of the conflict rise.

Such fragmentation, if it becomes visible, could significantly weaken regime cohesion and decision-making capacity.

Public unrest and protests

Domestic unrest represents one of the most significant internal challenges to regime stability.

Iran has experienced large-scale protests since late 2025, driven by economic crisis, inflation, and political dissatisfaction.

Key developments include:

  • Nationwide demonstrations across more than 100 cities
  • Escalation from economic grievances to anti-regime political demands
  • Violent crackdowns resulting in thousands of casualties

During the war, additional factors have intensified public pressure:

  • Economic hardship caused by sanctions and conflict disruption
  • Fear, uncertainty, and declining living conditions
  • Widespread internet restrictions limiting information flow

Although protests have been partially suppressed, underlying discontent remains unresolved, creating the potential for renewed instability.

Military vs political power shift

The war has accelerated a shift in power dynamics between military and civilian institutions.

In particular, the IRGC has expanded its role beyond defense into:

  • Internal security and protest suppression
  • Strategic decision-making during wartime
  • Control over key economic and political sectors

Reports of increased security deployments, checkpoints, and internal surveillance suggest a growing emphasis on militarized governance.

This shift reflects a broader pattern in conflict environments, where military institutions gain influence at the expense of civilian political authority.

Over time, such a transformation may entrench a more centralized and security-driven state structure, potentially reducing political flexibility and increasing authoritarian tendencies.

Analytical takeaway

The domestic dimension of the conflict reveals a complex balance between resilience and vulnerability. While the Iranian regime has maintained structural continuity through elite cohesion and coercive control, underlying pressures—ranging from public unrest to potential elite fragmentation—pose significant long-term risks. The increasing dominance of military institutions further reshapes the internal power structure, suggesting that regime stability, though preserved in the short term, remains deeply contingent on both internal dynamics and the trajectory of the war.

12. Humanitarian and Social Impact

Civilian casualties

The humanitarian cost of the conflict has been severe, with civilians bearing a disproportionate share of the damage.

Recent reports indicate that:

  • Over 1,900 people have been killed and tens of thousands injured since the start of the war ([Reuters][1])
  • A significant proportion of casualties are civilians, including women and children ([柳叶刀][2])
  • Strikes have affected densely populated urban areas, increasing the likelihood of collateral damage

High-profile incidents—including attacks on schools and hospitals—have raised serious concerns about compliance with international humanitarian law.

These patterns suggest that, despite claims of precision targeting, modern warfare continues to generate extensive civilian harm, particularly in urban environments.

Refugee crisis

The conflict has triggered a large-scale displacement crisis, both internally and across borders.

Key developments include:

  • Approximately 3.2 million people internally displaced within Iran
  • Pre-existing vulnerable populations, including over 1.6 million refugees, facing heightened risk
  • Neighboring countries preparing for potential cross-border refugee flows

In addition, millions of migrants—particularly Afghan populations in Iran—face renewed displacement and limited access to protection.

While large-scale cross-border movement remains limited so far, analysts warn that prolonged conflict could trigger a regional refugee crisis with significant political and economic consequences.

Infrastructure destruction

The war has caused widespread destruction of civilian infrastructure, severely affecting daily life and essential services.

Damage has included:

  • Tens of thousands of residential and public buildings destroyed
  • Hospitals, schools, and cultural sites directly impacted
  • Energy, water, and communication systems disrupted

By early stages of the conflict alone, thousands of civilian structures had already been damaged, with the number continuing to rise as hostilities persist.

This large-scale destruction has led to:

  • Shortages of electricity, food, and medical supplies
  • Environmental damage, including toxic pollution from industrial strikes
  • Long-term reconstruction challenges that may persist well beyond the conflict

Humanitarian aid

Humanitarian response efforts have expanded but remain constrained by access, security, and political challenges.

Key aspects include:

  • The Iranian Red Crescent serving as a primary nationwide relief provider
  • International organizations mobilizing emergency assistance and refugee support
  • Ongoing difficulties in delivering aid due to active conflict zones and sanctions-related restrictions

At the same time:

  • Medical systems are under severe strain due to mass casualties
  • Aid distribution is uneven, with some regions receiving limited support
  • Political tensions complicate coordination between international actors

These constraints highlight the gap between humanitarian needs and response capacity, particularly in high-intensity conflict environments.

Analytical takeaway

The humanitarian dimension of the conflict underscores the profound social consequences of modern warfare. Large-scale civilian casualties, mass displacement, and infrastructure destruction have combined to produce a rapidly escalating crisis, while humanitarian responses struggle to keep pace. As the conflict continues, the long-term social impact—ranging from demographic shifts to economic hardship and psychological trauma—may prove as significant as the immediate military outcomes.

13. Long-term Strategy and Future Order

Post-war Middle East order

The outcome of the conflict is likely to reshape the geopolitical landscape of the Middle East in profound ways.

Rather than restoring the previous status quo, analysts expect a reconfiguration of regional alignments and power balances. Key possibilities include:

  • A weakened but still resilient Iran retaining asymmetric influence
  • Increased security dependence of Gulf states on external powers
  • A more fragmented regional order with persistent instability

Recent discussions among Gulf states suggest that any post-war arrangement must go beyond ceasefire and address Iran’s long-term military capabilities, reflecting deep concerns about recurring conflict cycles.

Overall, the post-war order is unlikely to be stable; instead, it may institutionalize rivalry and periodic confrontation.

Security architecture

The war has exposed the limitations of the existing regional security framework, accelerating calls for a new security architecture.

Potential developments include:

  • Expanded U.S.-led or coalition-based defense systems in the Gulf
  • Increased military cooperation among regional states
  • Renewed discussions on collective security mechanisms

However, competing interests among major powers and regional actors complicate the creation of a unified system.

In practice, the future security architecture is likely to be fragmented and overlapping, with multiple security arrangements coexisting rather than a single integrated framework.

Arms race

The conflict is expected to intensify an already ongoing regional arms race.

Key dynamics include:

  • Increased investment in missile defense, drones, and advanced strike capabilities
  • Expansion of military budgets across Gulf states and Israel
  • Greater reliance on technological warfare, including cyber and AI-enabled systems

The presence of advanced weapon systems on multiple sides, combined with persistent security threats, creates a self-reinforcing cycle of militarization, where each actor’s defensive measures are perceived as offensive threats by others.

Such dynamics increase the likelihood of future crises and reduce incentives for de-escalation.

Nuclear proliferation risk

One of the most critical long-term concerns is the risk of nuclear proliferation in the region.

Although recent strikes have reportedly set back Iran’s nuclear program, they have not eliminated the underlying capability or knowledge base.

At the same time:

  • Iran still possesses enriched nuclear material with uncertain monitoring conditions
  • Military pressure may incentivize Iran to pursue nuclear deterrence more aggressively
  • Regional rivals may consider developing or acquiring nuclear capabilities in response

Scholars have long noted that existing nuclear asymmetries—particularly Israel’s undeclared arsenal—already contribute to proliferation pressures in the region.

Beyond direct weaponization, risks also include loss of control over nuclear materials or accidental escalation involving nuclear facilities.

Analytical takeaway

The long-term strategic outlook of the conflict suggests a transition toward a more unstable and competitive regional order. Rather than resolving underlying tensions, the war is likely to reinforce structural drivers of conflict, including security dilemmas, arms races, and proliferation risks.

As a result, the post-war Middle East may be characterized not by stability, but by a persistent state of strategic rivalry embedded within a fragmented and multipolar system.
